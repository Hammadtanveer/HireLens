Methodology

Two explainable signals, one score.

No black box: every candidate's score is the weighted sum of two numbers you can also see broken out individually on the results page.

A

JD relevance — 60%

A TF-IDF vector is built from the job description and every resume, then cosine similarity measures how closely each resume's overall language matches the JD's. This captures phrasing and context beyond a fixed keyword list — responsibilities, domain terms, tools mentioned in passing.

B

Skill coverage — 40%

Both the job description and each resume are scanned against a curated taxonomy of ~120 skills (languages, frameworks, cloud, data, soft skills). The score is the percentage of the JD's required skills that were actually found in the resume — shown to you as Matched and Missing chips.

C

Why weight it this way

Skill lists alone miss context — a resume can list "Python" once in a tools section with no depth. Pure text similarity alone can be fooled by resumes that are simply long or keyword-stuffed. Combining both means a resume has to be both textually relevant and actually cover the required skills to rank highly.

D

Honest limitations

This is a heuristic tool, not a hiring decision-maker. It can't judge quality of experience, verify claims, or catch skills phrased in ways outside the taxonomy. PDF text extraction can also struggle with heavily designed resume templates (multi-column layouts, text inside images). Always have a human review the shortlist.
